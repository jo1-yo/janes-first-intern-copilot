# Cold Emails to Founders

Small startups mostly never post intern openings — hiring an intern becomes real the moment a good candidate shows up in the founder's inbox. Founders read their own email. On a job board the user competes with 800 applicants; in the founder's inbox, they're often the only one who wrote. This is the highest-conversion channel in the playbook, and the one where concision matters most: a founder gives a cold email about 15 seconds.

## Picking the recipient

Match the recipient to the organization — the founder isn't always the right door:

- **Small startup (2–30 people):** the founder or hiring manager. They decide, and they read their own inbox.
- **Larger company:** a recruiter, an employee on the relevant team, or an alum from the user's school (alumni reply at far higher rates — search LinkedIn: company + school). Don't ask a stranger for a referral cold; ask a question or share proof first, and let the referral emerge.
- **Research role:** the professor, lab manager, or a graduate researcher in the lab. Cite the specific paper or project, not "your research."

Never claim familiarity with work the user hasn't actually read or used.

## Picking targets

Good targets share these traits:
- The user has actually used the product, or genuinely understands the space — this is what makes a real hook possible.
- Small (roughly 2–30 people): big enough to absorb an intern, small enough that the founder decides.
- Recently funded or recently launched: they have momentum and more work than people.
- Founder visible online (posting on X/LinkedIn, blog, podcast) — visible founders read cold email.

Finding the address: many founders list emails on their site, GitHub, or X profile; `firstname@company.com` is the common pattern. Use only public information — no scraping tools or paid lookups needed at this scale, and never be cagey about how you found them.

## The principle: clear beats clever

The email should read like a smart student wrote it in five honest minutes — not like a growth hacker engineered it. Founders reply to clarity and low friction, not to cleverness. There are two templates that work; pick by what the user has.

## Template A — the minimal fit email (~60 words, the default)

This exact structure landed the author of this playbook her internship. Use it when the user's background is an obvious fit for what the company does — the fit line does all the work a fancy hook would.

> **Subject:** Intern @ [Company]
>
> Hi [First name], I'm a [year] undergrad studying [X + Y], with a strong interest in [their space]. I've been following [Company] and am really excited about what you're building.
>
> I'm available [Month–Month] and [friction remover — see below].
>
> If you're open to it, I'd really appreciate the chance to chat. Thanks so much!

Four moves, one sentence each:
1. **Fit line** — identity + fields that visibly map onto the company's space ("cognitive science + CS, strong interest in AI filmmaking" to an AI film startup reads as *built for us*). This replaces the hook.
2. **Genuine interest** — one warm sentence, no essay. "I've been following X and am excited about what you're building" is enough when the fit line already proved relevance.
3. **Availability + friction remover** — specific dates plus something that makes the yes cheap: flexible on structure, part-time or full-time, remote or in person, open on compensation, or a spec trial project. (Offering to work unpaid is a real friction remover the original used — but check local law and norms first; unpaid internships at for-profit companies are restricted in many places, and flexible terms often remove just as much friction.)
4. **Zero-pressure ask** — "If you're open to it, I'd love to chat." Low pressure is not weakness; it makes replying effortless.

## Template B — the hook email (≤120 words)

Use when the user has a concrete proof point (an audience, a project, a number) or a real relationship with the product — a stronger claim deserves a stronger email.

1. **Hook — 1 sentence.** Something specific about *their* company that proves attention: a feature, a launch, a use case the user lived. The test: could this sentence be sent to any other company? If yes, it's not a hook.
2. **Who you are — 1–2 sentences.** One identity line plus ONE proof point with a number or a link. The strongest single proof, not three weak ones.
3. **Why them — 1 sentence.** Connect the proof to something the company visibly needs or is building. This is the sentence that turns "student wants job" into "person could help us."
4. **Small ask — 1 sentence.** Easy to say yes to: 15 minutes, or a specific tiny deliverable ("happy to mock up X to show what I'd do"). Never "do you have any openings?" — that's easy to say no to.

**Subject lines** (both templates): plain and clear wins. "Intern @ [Company]" is honest and works in a founder's inbox — this isn't an HR pipeline where you need to stand out from 800 subject lines. A concrete-curious subject ("20k-follower TikTok account, pointed at [company]?") also works when the email's best detail earns it. What fails is corporate-speak: ✗ "Opportunity to Connect" · ✗ "Passionate Student Seeking Experience".

## The kill list — instant archive

- Over ~150 words, or any paragraph over 3 lines
- Opening with the user's life story or major ("My name is X and I am a second-year student at...")
- Generic flattery ("your innovative company", "I've always admired...")
- Attachments in the first email (link a portfolio instead; attach the resume only when asked or when the ask is explicitly about a role)
- Vague asks ("any opportunities", "keep me in mind")
- Fake personalization — a mail-merged compliment reads as exactly what it is
- Apologizing for existing ("Sorry to bother you, I know you're incredibly busy...")

## The LinkedIn version

When the channel is LinkedIn instead of email (or as a parallel touch), compress further — a connection note caps at 300 characters, a DM should read in one glance:

> Hi [Name] — I've used [product] daily this semester and run a [proof, with number]. I'd love to intern with you this [season]; open to a quick chat? [link to work]

Same skeleton (fit/hook → proof → small ask), no salutation ceremony, no paragraphs. Send the email and the LinkedIn note to the *same* person only if the email got no reply — leading with both at once reads as a campaign.

## Follow-up cadence

- **Day 3–4:** two sentences, one NEW piece of information (something the user made, a genuinely useful thought about the product). Not "just bumping this."
- **Day 8–10:** final short note, warm close, door open.
- Then stop. Three messages total, ever. No guilt lines ("I guess you're not interested...").

Pre-write both follow-ups with the first email and put the dates in the tracker — follow-ups that aren't scheduled don't happen, and a large share of replies come from them.

## Scale with honesty

Reusing the user's own structure and proof sentences across emails is fine — that's the 80%. The hook and the ask must be genuinely specific to each company — that's the 20% that gets replies. If a real hook can't be written for a company, don't email that company today; email one where it can.

## How to run a cold-email session

1. Get the target company (or help pick targets using the criteria above) and the user's true proof points. If web access is available, research the company for hook material — recent launches, posts, funding. Only ever claim things about the user that they've confirmed.
2. Pick the template: strong concrete proof point or lived product relationship → Template B; otherwise → Template A (fit + friction removal). When in doubt, A — shorter and harder to get wrong.
3. Draft: subject + body (A: ~60 words, B: ≤120). Show the word count.
4. First time only, annotate: one line per sentence on why it's there (fit or hook / proof / why-them / ask), so the user learns the formula rather than depending on it.
5. Deliver the two follow-ups with send dates.
6. If they're emailing multiple companies, produce each email separately with a real fit line or hook — refuse the mail-merge shortcut, it converts to nothing.

## Full examples

**1 — Engineering student → dev-tools startup**

> **Subject:** your CLI's --watch flag saved my capstone
>
> Hi Maya — I used Flowdeck's --watch mode all semester for my capstone and ended up writing a small plugin that pipes its output into Notion (repo: github.com/user/flowdeck-notion, 40 stars).
>
> I'm a CS sophomore at UW. Since you launched integrations last month, I suspect there's a backlog of small connectors like this an intern could own end to end.
>
> Could I take 15 minutes to show you the plugin and hear what you'd hand an intern this summer?
>
> — Sam

**2 — Marketing student → consumer app**

> **Subject:** 20k-follower TikTok, pointed at Plately?
>
> Hi Jordan — I've logged 6 months of meals in Plately, and the "weekly plate" recap is the only app screen I've ever voluntarily shared to my story.
>
> I run a food TikTok with 20k followers (@user), and recaps like yours are exactly the format that travels there — but I don't see Plately on TikTok at all yet.
>
> I'd love to make you three sample TikToks on spec. If they work, let's talk about an internship; if not, you keep the videos.
>
> — Ana

**3 — Bad → good rewrite**

✗ *Before (98 words of nothing):*
> Dear Hiring Manager, My name is Kevin Liu and I am a second-year Business Administration student at State University. I am writing to express my sincere interest in any internship opportunities at your innovative company. I have always been passionate about technology and entrepreneurship, and I believe your company's mission aligns perfectly with my career goals. I am a hard-working, detail-oriented team player with excellent communication skills. Please find my resume attached. I would greatly appreciate the opportunity to discuss how I can contribute to your team. Thank you for your time and consideration.

✓ *After (58 words):*
> **Subject:** ran the numbers on Cartful's pricing page
>
> Hi Priya — I noticed Cartful's annual plan is priced below 10 of your 12 closest competitors; I put the comparison in a sheet (link).
>
> I'm a business sophomore who does this for fun. I'd happily do a proper pricing teardown as a trial project — one week, no strings.
>
> — Kevin
