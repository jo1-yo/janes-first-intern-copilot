# The Volume System

First-internship reply rates run 1–5% even with a strong resume. Ten applications is a coin flip; a hundred is a system. The goal of this workflow is to make volume sustainable and measurable, so silence becomes data instead of discouragement.

## The system

1. **Tracker — non-negotiable.** Create it from [assets/tracker-template.csv](../assets/tracker-template.csv), pre-filled with the user's first batch of real targets (do this for them; an empty template doesn't get used). Columns: Date, Company, Role, Link, Channel, Tier, Status, Follow-up date, Notes.

2. **Tier the targets:**
   - **A — dream / strong fit** (keep it ≤20% of the list): full resume tailoring + find a human and cold email them in addition to applying (see cold-email.md).
   - **B — solid fit**: keyword-tailoring pass on the resume, apply through the normal channel.
   - **C — volume**: base resume, apply fast, don't overthink. Perfectionism on C-tier applications is procrastination wearing a work costume.

3. **Daily quota, not weekly goals.** For example: 5–10 applications + 2 cold emails per day. Set it with the user based on their deadline (see below). Small and daily beats a weekend blitz that burns them out by Tuesday.

4. **Channels beyond the big boards** — posted intern roles are the minority, especially at small companies:
   - University career portal and department mailing lists (lowest competition per posting)
   - Startup job boards (YC's Work at a Startup, Wellfound, and local equivalents)
   - "We're hiring" posts on LinkedIn/X — reply fast, these move in days
   - Company career pages directly, for companies the user already admires
   - Professors, club alumni, anyone one degree away
   - Cold email to founders — the highest-conversion channel for small companies, covered in cold-email.md

5. **Read the funnel, don't guess.** Review the tracker with the user every week or two:
   - 50+ applications, ~0 replies → the resume is failing the 6-second test. Stop applying, go back to resume.md, fix it, then resume.
   - Replies but no interviews → the screening answers or short-answer questions are the leak; tighten those with the same concision rules.
   - Interviews but no offers → the materials are working; shift effort to interview practice (stories with numbers, same formula as resume bullets, spoken aloud).

6. **Follow-up:** one polite nudge 7–10 days after applying, only where a human contact exists. Skip it for faceless portals — put that energy into cold email instead.

## Finding live openings (and not fabricating them)

When web browsing is available, build the user a verified shortlist of 10–15 openings:

- Search for currently open roles matching the diagnosis (target role + adjacent roles), and prefer the employer's **official application page** over aggregator reposts.
- Verify each listing is still open, and check **mandatory** eligibility only: graduation window, academic level, location, major, work authorization. Don't disqualify the user over *preferred* qualifications — those are the employer's wish list, not a gate.
- Remove duplicates and obviously stale posts.
- For each listing include: company · title · location/remote · key eligibility requirements · why it matches this user (one sentence, citing their evidence) · priority (**Apply Now / Reach / Alternative**) · official link · date checked · best person to contact when identifiable.
- Never invent a position, deadline, recruiter, or link. Never call the list exhaustive — it's a starting shortlist, and the tracker is where it grows.

When browsing isn't available, say plainly that current openings can't be verified, and instead hand the user a repeatable search process: exact search queries ("graphic design intern" site:lever.co OR site:greenhouse.io), the company categories worth targeting, and the boards from the channel list above.

## Setting the quota from the deadline

Work backwards: `target volume ≈ deadline weeks × weekly capacity`, sanity-checked against the 1–5% reply rate. A user who needs an offer in 8 weeks should be aiming at 100–150 total applications plus 20–30 cold emails, which is ~3 applications and ~0.5 cold emails per day — very doable, and saying it this way makes it feel doable. Put the math in front of them.

## How to run a volume session

1. Build the tracker file with 10–20 real starter rows (research actual companies/roles matching their target if you can; otherwise have the user name their known targets and fill the rest by channel).
2. Agree on the daily quota and write it into the plan with actual dates.
3. Offer batch-tailoring: "paste today's 5 job descriptions and I'll produce 5 keyword passes of your resume." This is where an assistant removes the real bottleneck — tailoring time.
4. Schedule the funnel review: after every ~25 applications, look at the numbers together and adjust the leak, not everything at once.
